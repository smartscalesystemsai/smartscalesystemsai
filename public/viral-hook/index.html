Why the first three seconds decide everything

On Shorts, Reels and TikTok, most viewers leave before the third second. The algorithm reads that as a signal: if people swipe away immediately, it stops showing the video. A strong opening line doesn't just win attention — it buys the distribution that everything else depends on.

This is why two videos with identical content can perform a hundred times apart. The difference is rarely the edit or the topic. It's the first sentence.

What actually makes a hook work

Hooks that hold attention tend to do one of four things:

  • Open a loop. Raise a question the viewer needs answered. "I spent $4,000 finding this out so you don't have to" works because the payoff is withheld.
  • Contradict an assumption. "Posting daily is why your channel is stuck" stops the scroll because it argues with something the viewer already believes.
  • Name the audience. "If you edit your own videos, this will save you six hours a week" filters instantly — the right people lean in.
  • Show the result first. Lead with the outcome, then explain how. Curiosity about the method keeps people watching.

How to use this tool

Paste a topic, a YouTube URL, or a full script. Pick your platform — short-form and long-form reward different openings, since a Short has three seconds and a YouTube video has closer to fifteen. You'll get five hooks to choose from.

Treat them as drafts, not final copy. The best results come from picking the angle that fits and rewriting it in your own voice. A hook that sounds like you will always outperform one that sounds like software.

Common mistakes

Warming up. "Hey guys, welcome back to the channel" spends your most valuable three seconds on nothing. Start at the point.

Over-promising. A hook that oversells what follows gets the click and loses the watch time. Retention matters more than the first second.

Writing the hook last. If you write it first, it shapes the whole video around one clear idea — and the video gets better, not just the opening.
