Why a small stack beats a big one

The most common mistake is subscribing to five tools that each solve a slice of the same problem. You pay five times, learn five interfaces, and still hand-carry work between them. A stack earns its place when each tool removes a step you were doing manually — not when it adds a step you now have to manage.

That's why every recommendation here starts with the free option. If the free tier clears the bottleneck, you've solved it for nothing. Paid tools are worth it when the constraint is throughput rather than capability: you already know the job, you just can't do it fast enough by hand.

How to read the time estimate

The hours-saved figure assumes you already know your workflow and are replacing manual steps, not learning the craft from scratch. Expect the first week to be slower, not faster — you're setting things up. The saving shows up from about week two, once the setup is done and you're running the process rather than building it.

Treat it as a rough order of magnitude. Saving four hours a week and saving forty are different decisions; saving four versus five is noise.

Start with one

Pick the single bottleneck costing you the most hours and fix only that. Changing one workflow at a time means you can tell what actually worked. Change four at once and you'll never know which one mattered — or which one to undo when something breaks.
